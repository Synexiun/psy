# Urge surfing

Urge surfing is one of the most useful skills in this app and one of the most studied.

## The idea

An urge is a wave. It rises, it peaks, and — if you do not act on it — it falls. Most urges crest within about 20 minutes. The common experience is that the urge feels *permanent* while you are in it, and *only in retrospect* does it look like a wave.

Urge surfing is the practice of staying with the wave until it passes, without fighting it, without acting on it.

## The evidence

Urge surfing comes from Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP), developed by Alan Marlatt, Sarah Bowen, and colleagues at the University of Washington. Controlled trials of MBRP have shown reduced relapse rates in substance-use contexts compared with treatment as usual (Witkiewitz, Bowen, Harrop et al., 2014). The mechanism — experiencing a craving without reactivity — is consistent with broader research on mindfulness and emotion regulation.

## How to do it

You can do this with the audio guide in the app, or from memory.

1. **Notice.** Without any agenda to change anything, notice the urge. Where in your body is it? What is the sensation like — tight, warm, restless, heavy?

2. **Don't fight.** Resisting an urge tends to amplify it. So does pushing it away. For the next few minutes, you are not resisting and not acting. You are just *observing*.

3. **Describe.** In your head, or out loud, name what is there. "I feel a tightening in my chest. I have a thought that says *I need a drink*. My hands feel restless."

4. **Stay with it.** Breathe slowly. If you get pulled into a story about the urge — *I always lose, this always wins, I cannot do this* — gently notice you are in the story, and come back to the sensations.

5. **Watch it change.** Urges do not stay at the peak. They move. Watch yours move. This is the surfing part.

## What to expect

The first few times, it may feel like the urge lasts forever. It will feel very strong. The feeling that you *have to* do something is itself the urge talking.

What you are building is a track record in your own mind: *I have had urges this strong, and I did not act on them, and they passed.* Each one of those is a real data point, and they accumulate.

## When to use it

- When an urge is rising. This is what it is designed for.
- When an urge is peaking. Hardest time — also the most valuable practice.
- Not so much when you are already past the urge and the wave has already broken. Other tools are better for that part.

## When not to use it

- If the urge is tied to a crisis-level state — thoughts of self-harm, or a situation that is more than a craving for a behavior. For those, tap SOS and let the app route you to the right place, or see [Safety resources](13_safety_resources.md).

## A note on compassion

Do not treat urge surfing as a test you can fail. Even if you notice a thought like *I am so bad at this*, notice it and come back. That thought is part of the urge. The practice is simply staying present. That is it. That is the whole skill.

## Your personal data

Every time you use urge surfing, you can tell the app afterward how it went. *Helped. Neutral. Didn't help. Made worse.* Over time the app learns when urge surfing is the best tool for you in a given context, and when another tool might be better. Your feedback is how that happens.
