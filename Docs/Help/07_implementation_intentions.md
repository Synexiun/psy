# Your if-then plans (implementation intentions)

If there is one technique from psychology research that punches above its weight, it is this one.

An **if-then plan** — what researchers call an *implementation intention* — is a small rule you write for yourself, once, in advance, that takes the form *"If X happens, I will do Y."*

That is it. It sounds trivially simple. It is not. It is one of the most well-replicated findings in behavioral research.

## The evidence

The term was introduced by Peter Gollwitzer in the late 1990s. A 2006 meta-analysis by Gollwitzer and Sheeran pooled 94 independent studies across education, health, work, and everyday tasks. The average effect size was Cohen's d ≈ 0.65 — a medium-to-large effect, larger than goal-setting alone.

In plainer language: people who wrote down an if-then plan *in advance* were substantially more likely to actually do the thing they had planned when the moment came, compared with people who had the same intention but had not specified an if-then.

## Why it works

When you make a decision in the moment, you are doing it with all of the cognitive constraints of that moment — you are tired, or stressed, or already partway down a path. Your willpower, such as it is, has to push against all of that.

When you decide *in advance* — under calmer, clearer conditions — you are specifying a rule that only needs to be *recognized* in the moment, not decided. Your brain does not have to compute "what do I do?" It just has to notice the trigger and execute.

It is the difference between writing code and running code. If the code is already written, running it is fast and reliable.

## How to write one

A good if-then plan has three parts:

1. **A specific trigger.** Not *"when I feel stressed."* That is too vague. Something like *"when I walk in the door after work"* or *"when my colleague Jim offers me a drink."*

2. **A specific action.** Not *"be strong."* That is not actionable. *"I will take off my jacket, put my keys in the bowl, and do box breathing for one minute."* That, you can do.

3. **A single chain, not a tree.** Do not try to plan for all branches at once. One trigger, one action.

## Examples

- *"If I pass the corner store on my way home, I will put my earbuds in and call my brother."*
- *"If I am about to open Instagram, I will close the app and drink a glass of water."*
- *"If I feel the urge after 6pm on a Friday, I will open the app and tap SOS."*
- *"If I wake up anxious, I will do a 60-second body scan before I look at my phone."*

## How the app uses them

When you build a plan in the app, it becomes part of your personal library. The app surfaces the relevant plan in real time when the trigger context is active — based on time, location, biometric signal, or a pattern you've established.

You will also see your plans when you open the tool library. You can edit or retire any of them at any time. If a plan is not working for you, retire it and write a different one. This is not a commitment device; it is a tool.

## Tips

- **Keep them short.** A plan you remember matters more than a plan that is comprehensive.
- **Start with one.** You do not need twenty. You need one that fires reliably. Add more later.
- **Review them.** Every week or so, look through your plans. Keep what works. Retire what does not. Write new ones for the patterns you are noticing.
- **Write them in your own words.** If-then plans work better when they sound like you.

## A common mistake

A plan like *"if I feel an urge, I will not give in"* is not an if-then plan. It is a goal. The *then* part is a negation, not an action.

A better version: *"if I feel an urge, I will open the app and do urge surfing."* Now the *then* is a concrete thing to do. Your brain has something to execute.

Actions are easier to execute than abstentions. Build your plans that way.
