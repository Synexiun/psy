# Assessments explained

Discipline OS uses a small set of validated questionnaires to track how you are doing over time. These are not tests. There is no grade. What they give you — and, if you share, your clinician — is a useful, research-based picture of change.

## The assessments we use

### PHQ-9

Nine questions about depression symptoms over the last two weeks. Published in 2001 by Kroenke, Spitzer, and Williams. Scored 0–27. It is probably the most-used depression screening instrument in the world.

The PHQ-9 has one item about thoughts of self-harm. If your answer to that item is anything other than "not at all," the app will route you to safety resources, because that is what the instrument is designed to do. This is not a surprise; it is why the instrument works.

Translations we use: validated versions in English, French, Arabic (AlHadi et al., 2017), and Persian (Dadfar & Kabir, 2016).

### GAD-7

Seven questions about anxiety symptoms over the last two weeks. Published in 2006 by Spitzer, Kroenke, Williams, and Löwe. Scored 0–21.

### AUDIT / AUDIT-C

Ten questions about alcohol use (full AUDIT) or three questions for quick screening (AUDIT-C). Developed and maintained by the World Health Organization. Validated across many languages.

### DAST-10

Ten yes/no questions about drug use. Used where non-alcohol substance use is present.

### PSS-10

Ten questions about how stressful life has felt in the last month. Published in 1983 by Sheldon Cohen.

### WHO-5

Five short questions about subjective wellbeing. A WHO instrument since 1998. One of the most brief and most-validated wellbeing measures.

### DTCQ-8

Eight questions about how confident you feel refraining from substance use in eight different situations. Developed by Sklar and Turner in 1999. Where a validated translation is not available (at launch, Arabic and Persian do not have well-validated DTCQ-8 translations), we substitute a general self-efficacy measure.

### URICA and the Readiness Ruler

Questions about where you are in the change process — from "I'm not really sure this is a problem" all the way through to "I'm actively working on it and maintaining it." These help the app calibrate what kind of support fits best.

### C-SSRS

This one is different. The C-SSRS is the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, a standardized clinical instrument. It is not administered on a schedule — it is only used when something in the conversation or content suggests that it should be, or if you request it. Its presence or absence does not predict anything about you; it is a careful clinical tool used when it is useful.

## What the scores mean

For each instrument, the app shows your score alongside:

- What the score means, in plain language (not just a number).
- Whether your change since last time is **meaningful** or **not yet meaningful**. We use a statistical rule called the **Reliable Change Index** (Jacobson & Truax, 1991) to distinguish real change from measurement noise. If your PHQ-9 moved from 11 to 9, that is within the range of normal test-to-test variation; the app will show you the movement but will not dramatize it. If it moved from 11 to 5, that is outside the noise range and we'll mark it as a reliable change.

This is deliberate. Small movements look meaningful if you squint; they often are not. We wait for the data to actually say something before saying something.

## How your clinician sees these

If you have a clinician and you have linked them with share permission that includes trajectories, they see your scores over time with the reference ranges and the reliable-change markers that are standard in clinical literature. They see exactly what a clinical researcher would see, not a product-manager version of it.

What they do not see, by default, is anything from your journal or voice notes unless you have given them explicit access to a specific entry.

## Cadence

- PHQ-9, GAD-7, WHO-5: every two weeks.
- AUDIT-C, PSS-10, DTCQ-8: every four weeks.
- URICA: monthly.
- Readiness Ruler: weekly, a lightweight one-item check.
- PHQ-2 and GAD-2 (shorter versions): weekly, as gating screeners.
- C-SSRS: only when triggered.

You can pause any of these except the safety-linked items (PHQ-9 item 9, C-SSRS). If you do not want those administered, you can pause assessments entirely; we do not silently skip the safety routing.

## What we don't do

- We do not use these scores to market to you.
- We do not sell them anywhere.
- We do not use them to rank you against other users.
- We do not attempt to diagnose you. These are screening instruments; a diagnosis is something a clinician can do, not an app.

## Why this matters

Most mental-health apps show you a mood trend with a number that is not grounded in any particular instrument. Those numbers feel precise but are not validated. A 7.3 on some proprietary "wellness score" is not a thing that has been tested against anything.

The instruments used here have been tested — across thousands of people, across decades, across many languages. When you see your PHQ-9 trajectory, you are looking at something that a psychiatrist at any major academic hospital would recognize, because it is what they use too.

We chose that because it is what is actually useful. If your picture is ever going to connect back to clinical care — through your own clinician, through an enterprise health benefit, through a future research effort — it needs to be in a language that connects.
