# Box breathing

Box breathing is a 90-second breathing pattern. It is the fastest way in this app to change how you feel in your body, with nothing more than your attention.

## The pattern

Four equal parts, each four seconds long:

1. Breathe **in** for 4 seconds.
2. Hold for 4 seconds.
3. Breathe **out** for 4 seconds.
4. Hold for 4 seconds.

Then repeat. Four to six rounds is usually enough — that is about 90 seconds.

The app will guide you with a small expanding and contracting square on screen, and with audio if you have it on. You can also just close your eyes and count.

## Why it works

Your breath is one of the only direct levers you have on your autonomic nervous system. A slow, regular, paced breath sends a signal through the vagus nerve to your heart and brain that you are not in danger. Your heart rate variability increases, your heart rate steadies, and the part of your brain that handles reactive emotion gets a little more bandwidth.

Box breathing specifically — with holds between inhales and exhales — gives the body a moment at each transition to settle. It is a standard tool in high-stress professional settings; it is used by military and first-responder training programs for exactly this reason.

## When to use it

- Before a hard conversation.
- When an urge is starting to rise but has not yet peaked.
- Between meetings, as a reset.
- At the start of a daily check-in, to land yourself.
- As the first thing you do if you notice anxiety climbing.

## When something else might be better

- If the distress is very high, [TIPP](03_tipp.md) works faster — specifically the cold-on-face version.
- If the issue is a persistent thought rather than body activation, [cognitive defusion](06_cognitive_defusion.md) fits better.
- If you are in crisis, do not start with breathing. Go to [Safety resources](13_safety_resources.md).

## Tips

- **Breathe through your nose if you can.** Nose breathing slows the pace naturally and engages the diaphragm more.
- **Soften your shoulders.** Most of us carry tension there. Notice, release.
- **Do not force the holds.** If four seconds feels long, start with three. The pattern matters more than the exact numbers.
- **Do not chase outcomes.** This is a tool, not a test. The *doing* is the whole point; whether you feel dramatically different by the end is not what determines whether it was worthwhile.

## Variations

- **If 4-4-4-4 feels short:** try 5-5-5-5 or 6-6-6-6. The relaxing effect tends to increase with longer counts, up to a limit.
- **If the holds are hard:** drop them and use [4-7-8 breathing](05_breathing_478.md) instead, which has no hold on the exhale side.

## What you will notice

After four to six rounds, most people feel a slight shift. Shoulders lower. Jaw softens. Thoughts have a little more room. This is not a dramatic transformation — it is a small, reliable move toward steadier ground.

That is often enough to change what happens next.
