# TIPP — when it's very intense

TIPP is for the moments when your body is in the red zone and you need the distress level to come down *fast* so you can think again. It is the most physical of the tools in this app, and it works by taking advantage of the way your body's stress response is wired.

## What the letters mean

- **T** — Temperature. Cold on your face.
- **I** — Intense exercise. Brief, hard movement.
- **P** — Paced breathing. Breathe out longer than you breathe in.
- **P** — Paired muscle relaxation. Tense and release, methodically.

You do not need to do all four. One is often enough. In the app, TIPP presents them in order of fastest effect.

## The evidence

TIPP comes from Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), developed by Marsha Linehan at the University of Washington. It is one of the most physically grounded skills in the DBT distress-tolerance module. It is widely used in clinical settings for acute distress, and there is consistent research supporting its mechanism via the parasympathetic nervous system — the "rest and digest" side of your autonomic nervous system.

## How to do each one

### Temperature (fastest)

Put something very cold on your face. A cold pack wrapped in a thin cloth. A splash of cold water. A bag of frozen peas from the freezer. The trick is to engage what physiologists call the **dive reflex** — the involuntary response that slows your heart rate when your face gets cold.

Hold it on your face — especially the area just below your eyes and above your cheekbones — for about 30 seconds.

You will feel a shift. Your heart rate will come down. Your body is doing this on its own, and you are just letting it.

One safety note: if you have a cardiac condition or are pregnant, cold-induced vasoconstriction can have other effects — talk to your clinician about whether this is a tool for you.

### Intense exercise (also fast)

Any hard physical effort for 60 to 90 seconds. Jumping jacks. Running up and down stairs. Fast push-ups. A hard walk if you are not in a state to do more.

The point is not fitness. The point is to spend the adrenaline your body has already made. Distress produces stress hormones; exercise burns them. After the burst, you will feel a different kind of tired — the kind that lets your nervous system reset.

### Paced breathing (works anywhere)

Breathe out longer than you breathe in. A simple pattern: in for 4, out for 6 or 8. Do this for two to three minutes.

When your exhale is longer than your inhale, you are stimulating the vagus nerve, which shifts your body out of fight-or-flight. This is the same mechanism behind [box breathing](04_box_breathing.md) and [4-7-8](05_breathing_478.md).

### Paired muscle relaxation

Tense a muscle group hard for 5 seconds, then fully release. Work your way through: hands, arms, shoulders, face, stomach, legs, feet. Feel the contrast between tension and release.

This one takes the longest — about 5 to 10 minutes — but the residue is a much lower baseline.

## When to use TIPP

- Intense anger, before it drives a decision you'll regret.
- A craving that feels overwhelming and other tools have not helped.
- Panic or panic-adjacent states.
- When your body is so activated you cannot think.

## When not to use TIPP

- If the situation is crisis-level — thoughts of self-harm or worse. Go to [Safety resources](13_safety_resources.md) or tap SOS; TIPP is powerful but it is not enough for crisis moments.
- For long-term emotional regulation. TIPP is an acute tool. For ongoing work, longer practices like [body scan](08_body_scan.md) or [self-compassion](10_self_compassion.md) serve better.

## After TIPP

Once your body is less activated, you can think more clearly. This is often the right moment to do a short reflection — a check-in, a journal entry, or a brief talk with someone — while the insight is fresh and the fog of distress has lifted.
