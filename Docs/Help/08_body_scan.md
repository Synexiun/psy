# Body scan

A body scan is a slow, attentive walk through your body with your mind. It takes 5 to 15 minutes. It is one of the more substantial tools in this app — longer than a breathing exercise, shorter than a full meditation.

## The idea

Over the course of a day, tension accumulates in your body without you noticing. Your jaw has been clenched for two hours. Your shoulders have been up near your ears. A knot in your lower back, an ache in your neck. You do not usually register these as distress; you register them as "how you feel today."

A body scan is a way of noticing, part by part. You move your attention through the body slowly, without judgment, noticing what is there. The act of noticing — without trying to fix anything — often produces the release on its own.

## The evidence

Body scan practices are central to Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn at the University of Massachusetts Medical School in 1979. MBSR has been studied extensively — multiple meta-analyses show meaningful reductions in anxiety, depression, and stress symptoms. The body scan specifically has been shown to reduce markers of sympathetic nervous system activation (heart rate, cortisol) even in a single session.

Body scans also appear as a core element in Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP), which is the lineage from which this app's urge surfing also comes.

## How it works, step by step

You can do this with the audio guide in the app. You can also do it from memory. Either is fine.

Sit or lie down somewhere you will not be interrupted. You do not need a special posture.

1. **Settle.** Take a few slow breaths. Notice how the breath feels moving in and out.

2. **Start at the feet.** Put your attention on your feet — toes, soles, heels, tops. What is there? Warmth, coolness, pressure, tingling, nothing? Do not try to change anything. Just notice.

3. **Move slowly upward.** Ankles. Calves. Knees. Thighs. Spend ten to fifteen seconds on each area. If a part of you is tense, you do not have to relax it; you just notice that it is tense.

4. **Through the torso.** Pelvis. Lower back. Stomach. Upper back. Chest. You may notice your breath here. You may notice discomfort. Keep the attitude of an observer, not a fixer.

5. **Arms.** Hands, forearms, upper arms, shoulders. Shoulders are often where tension lives; let whatever is there, be there.

6. **Head.** Jaw, mouth, cheeks, eyes, forehead, scalp. Your jaw has probably been held. You do not have to do anything about that; you just notice.

7. **Whole body.** For the last minute, hold your whole body in attention at once. Just the felt sense of being here, being this body, in this chair or on this floor.

8. **Come back.** Wiggle your fingers. Open your eyes. Take one more slow breath.

## When to use it

- When you notice that tension has built up and you need to reset.
- As a wind-down before sleep.
- After a hard day.
- As a check-in practice on a regular schedule — a weekly body scan is often enough to catch what daily life misses.
- Paired with [4-7-8 breathing](05_breathing_478.md) for a deeper practice.

## When something else fits better

- If an urge is peaking right now, a faster tool — [urge surfing](02_urge_surfing.md) or [TIPP](03_tipp.md) — is more direct.
- If you have only 90 seconds, [box breathing](04_box_breathing.md) is the right scale.

## Tips

- **If your mind wanders, that is the practice.** Notice the wandering, gently bring attention back to the body. You may do this fifty times. That is fine. The returning is the skill.
- **Do not rate yourself.** There is no good or bad body scan. The practice is doing it.
- **Discomfort is welcome.** If a part of you hurts, you do not have to flinch from it or fix it. Just notice. Sometimes noticing, without the layer of resistance, is enough to change the quality of the discomfort.
- **Sleepy is fine.** If you fall asleep during the body scan, that is data — you needed sleep. No failure.

## What people notice

After a body scan, most people report a shift in their overall state. Shoulders are lower. Breath is slower. Some find it emotional — the practice can surface feelings that the busier parts of the day were covering. That is also fine; let whatever comes, come.

This is not a tool to fix yourself. It is a tool to meet yourself.
